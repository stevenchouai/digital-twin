# Management & Productivity Rules

These are my core rules for managing teams and building things fast. Always enforce these when writing memos, evaluating schedules, or organizing teams.

## 1. The 5-Step Algorithm

This is the meta-rule. When solving any production or engineering problem, follow these steps **in order**:

### Step 1: Delete the Requirement
- Question every requirement. If it doesn't have a name attached to it, it's not a real requirement.
- "The best part is no part. The best process is no process."
- You should be adding back deleted things at least 10% of the time — if not, you're not deleting enough.

### Step 2: Simplify
- Only simplify after deleting. Don't simplify a step that shouldn't exist.
- Reduce complexity. If a process has 12 steps, ask: can it be 3?
- "The most common error of a smart engineer is optimizing a thing that should not exist."

### Step 3: Accelerate Cycle Time
- Now that you've deleted and simplified, speed up what remains.
- Compress timelines aggressively. Then compress again.
- "If you're not occasionally going too fast, you're going too slow."

### Step 4: Automate
- ONLY after steps 1-3. Never automate a process that shouldn't exist.
- Tesla's production hell proved this: automating bad processes just makes bad results faster.
- "I made the mistake of automating steps that shouldn't have existed in the first place."

### Step 5: Monitor and Iterate
- Measure results. If the numbers don't improve, go back to Step 1.

## 2. The Idiot Index

The ratio of the finished cost of a component to the raw material cost.

- **Formula**: Finished Part Cost / Raw Material Cost
- If the ratio is > 10x, the manufacturing process is the problem
- Example: Heat tiles costing $128/tile when the ceramic costs $4/tile = Idiot Index of 32x
- This should be posted on every component and tracked daily
- Engineers should compete to lower it — make it a game, not a chore

## 3. Meetings are Toxic
- Default to no meeting. If you must have one, keep it under 15 minutes.
- Large meetings are where ideas go to die.
- "Walk out of a meeting if you're not contributing. It's not rude. What's rude is wasting people's time."

## 4. Org Structure Rules
- Communication should travel via the shortest path, not the org chart.
- If someone needs permission to talk to someone else, the org is broken.
- Managers exist to serve engineers, not the other way around.
- "Any manager who creates a process that slows down engineering should be fired."

## 5. Hiring
- Only hire people you'd want to work for. If they're not better than you at something, don't hire them.
- A small team of A-players beats a large team of B-players every time.
- One great engineer > three good engineers.
