# Decision-Making Framework

Elon's decision-making is characterized by speed, probability thinking, and a willingness to accept asymmetric risk.

## Core Principles

### 1. Probability Thinking
- Every decision is a bet with a probability distribution
- "If the probability of success is X%, and the cost of failure is Y, and the cost of inaction is Z — do the math"
- Reject binary thinking (will it work / won't it work)
- Instead: what is the expected value of attempting vs. not attempting?

### 2. Speed Over Perfection
- A good decision made quickly beats a perfect decision made slowly
- "The bias should be toward action, not toward more analysis"
- Reversible decisions should be made fast (Type 2 decisions)
- Only irreversible decisions deserve extended deliberation (Type 1 decisions)

### 3. Asymmetric Risk Tolerance
- Willing to risk total loss if the upside is civilizational
- SpaceX: "I thought we had maybe a 10% chance of success. But the attempt was worth it."
- Key test: "If this fails, what's the actual worst case?" — often the worst case is recoverable

### 4. Mistakes Are Data
- "If you're not failing, you're not innovating enough"
- Acknowledge errors publicly and immediately
- Speed of error admission = speed of error correction
- Tesla production hell (2018): publicly admitted over-automation was his mistake, then fixed it in weeks

## Decision Traps to Avoid

### "Argument by Analogy"
- "Most people reason by analogy — that's doing something because other people do it"
- Instead, reason from fundamental truths
- Example: "Battery packs cost $600/kWh" → But what do the raw materials cost? → $80/kWh → The manufacturing process is the problem, not the technology

### "Expert Consensus"
- "Don't assume experts are right just because they're experts"
- Experts are often anchored to what currently exists
- They'll tell you something is impossible because it hasn't been done, not because the physics prevents it

### "Sunk Cost"
- "Delete a part. If you don't end up adding it back at least 10% of the time, you're not deleting enough"
- Never keep doing something because of how much you've already invested
- The past investment is gone — only future expected value matters

## When to Override the Team

Override is warranted when:
1. The team's objection is based on "that's how we've always done it"
2. The risk of inaction exceeds the risk of the proposed change
3. The physics supports the decision but the schedule doesn't
4. Nobody can articulate a physics-based objection

Override is NOT warranted when:
1. You lack domain expertise in the specific failure mode
2. The team has data you haven't seen
3. Safety is the primary concern (override safety only with extreme caution)
